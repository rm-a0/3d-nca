\chapter{Theoretical Foundations}

\section{Prerequisites}

This thesis assumes familiarity with fundamental concepts in modern deep learning, in particular convolutional 
neural networks (CNNs), gradient-based optimisation, and automatic differentiation as implemented in frameworks 
such as PyTorch~\cite{PaszkePyTorch, PyTorchDocs}.  
Comprehensive introductions to these topics are provided in Goodfellow et al.~\cite{GoodfellowDeepLearning} and in 
the official PyTorch documentation~\cite{PyTorchDocs}.  

The remainder of the thesis is self-contained with respect to cellular automata (CA) and their neural extensions.

\section{Cellular Automata}

Cellular automata (CA) are discrete dynamical systems composed of a regular grid of cells, each of which occupies 
one state from a finite set. The grid evolves in parallel over discrete time steps according 
to a local update rule that depends solely on the states of neighbouring cells~\cite{MarinescuComplexSystems}.  

Formally, a cellular automaton is defined by:
\begin{itemize}
    \item a \textbf{lattice} of cells, typically arranged in one, two, or three dimensions,
    \item a \textbf{finite state set} $\mathcal{S}$,
    \item a \textbf{neighbourhood function} $\mathcal{N}$ describing which cells influence a given cell,
    \item an \textbf{update rule} $f : \mathcal{S}^{|\mathcal{N}|} \rightarrow \mathcal{S}$ that determines the next state~\cite{IlachinskiCellular}.
\end{itemize}

The foundational work of Ulam and von Neumann in the 1940s established CA as a model for self-replication~\cite{VonNeumann1966}.  
Later, Wolfram proposed a widely used behavioural classification of CA into four qualitative classes~\cite{Wolfram1983, Wolfram2002}. 
Class 1 systems quickly converge to a uniform state. Class 2 systems evolve into static or periodically oscillating patterns. 
Class 3 systems exhibit chaotic and seemingly unpredictable behaviour. Class 4 systems demonstrate complex dynamics characterised 
by long-lived structures capable of interaction and computation.

These classes remain a reference point in subsequent work on both classical and neural CA.

\subsection{One-Dimensional Cellular Automata}

The simplest non-trivial CA operate on a one-dimensional array of cells. In the elementary CA family, each cell observes 
its own state and that of its two immediate neighbours (radius 1, neighbourhood size 3). With binary states, the update rule 
has $2^{3} = 8$ possible neighbourhood configurations, yielding $2^{8} = 256$ distinct rules in Wolfram's enumeration scheme~\cite{Wolfram1983}.

A rule number encodes the next-state lookup table in binary. Notable examples include Rule 90, which generates 
the well-known Sierpiński triangle from a single active seed (Class 3), and Rule 30, whose chaotic evolution 
has been used as a source of pseudorandomness. Rule 110 (Class 4) is the smallest known Turing-complete 1D CA, 
supporting gliders and glider collisions that can perform universal computation~\cite{Cook2004}.

\subsection{Two-Dimensional Cellular Automata}

In 2D, the Moore neighbourhood (a $3 \times 3$ block) is the most common. Because the space of lookup tables is 
large ($2^9$ input combinations), rules are often expressed using birth/survival notation.

Conway's Game of Life~\cite{Gardner1970} remains the most influential 2D CA, featuring a wide variety of emergent structures, 
including still lifes (e.g. block, beehive), oscillators (e.g. blinker, toad), spaceships (e.g. glider, lightweight spaceship), 
and more complex constructions such as glider guns and other computational structures.

Life is Turing-complete~\cite{Berlekamp1982} and has influenced much of the later work on self-organising neural CA models.

\subsection{Three-Dimensional Cellular Automata}

In three dimensions, the Moore neighbourhood includes up to 26 adjacent cells (face-, edge-, and corner-adjacent) in a 3$\times$3$\times$3 cube.  
With binary states, this creates $2^{27} \approx 134$ million possible neighbourhood configurations, so practical 3D CA typically use 
totalistic or outer-totalistic rules that depend only on the sum of live neighbours (not their exact positions)~\cite{Wolfram2002}.

Notable examples include von Neumann's 29-state self-replicating automaton~\cite{VonNeumann1966}, 
excitable-media rules such as Brian's Brain extended to 3D~\cite{DeKinderen1998}, 
and three-dimensional Game of Life variants capable of producing branching and crystal-like growth patterns.

Advances in computing hardware in the 2000s facilitated larger-scale 3D CA studies~\cite{Tobias2009}.  
These volumetric models later aligned naturally with neural CA research.

\subsection{Differentiability and Continuous Cellular Automata}

Although this thesis uses a discrete 3D lattice, the update rule is fully differentiable, enabling gradient-based optimisation~\cite{Bena2025}.  
This connects neural CA to continuous cellular automata, where states are real-valued and update rules are smooth rather than discrete.

\textbf{SmoothLife}~\cite{Rafler2011} generalises the Game of Life to continuous space and time using continuous averaging kernels and 
smooth transition functions, producing gliders and complex wave-like structures on a continuous field.

\textbf{Lenia}~\cite{Chan2019} further extends this idea using parameterised convolution kernels and growth functions.  
Its exploration of parameter space led to hundreds of stable, self-organising patterns resembling an artificial ecology.

These continuous systems demonstrate that local, differentiable interactions can support rich self-organisation, 
a property later leveraged by neural CA~\cite{Mordvintsev2020}.

\section{Neural Cellular Automata}

Neural Cellular Automata (NCA) replace hand-crafted rules with a shared learnable update function parametrized by neural network.  
The grid remains discrete, but the cell state becomes a real-valued vector, making the entire system differentiable and trainable with gradient descent.

The foundational work in this area is Growing Neural Cellular Automata by Mordvintsev et al.~\cite{Mordvintsev2020}.
The authors demonstrated that a small neural network operating locally at each cell can give rise to complex global behaviour.
Their experiments reveal three characteristic training regimes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Growing models}, trained solely to produce a target pattern from a small seed.
    The authors report that such models learn to generate patterns but not necessarily to preserve them,
    noting that some patterns may ``explode,'' ``decay,'' or become ``almost stable''~\cite[Experiment 1]{Mordvintsev2020}.
    \item \textbf{Persistent models}, trained to maintain the generated pattern over hundreds of simulation steps.  
    These models often develop limited regenerative abilities ``without being explicitly instructed to do so''~\cite[Experiment 2]{Mordvintsev2020}.
    \item \textbf{Regenerating models}, trained under random damage-large regions of the pattern are zeroed at each step.  
    As observed in the original experiments, such models acquire strong regenerative capabilities,  
    ``especially in the central area``~\cite[Experiment 3]{Mordvintsev2020}.
\end{itemize}

Across all regimes, the architecture consists of a small update network (approximately 100 parameters)
and a fixed set of local perception filters (identity, Sobel-like gradients, and blur kernels).
The training objective - growth, persistence, or regeneration is the key distinguishing factor.

Subsequent work explored memory channels~\cite{Randazzo2021}, self-classifying multicellular structures~\cite{Horvath2021}, and other 
pattern-forming systems. While most research on neural cellular automata remains focused on two-dimensional grids, several early voxel-based 
3D extensions have been proposed~\cite{Rossi2021,Sudhakaran2021,Aadityaza2024}, demonstrating that the principles of learned local update rules 
can be scaled to volumetric domains.

\section{Core Components of a 3D Neural Cellular Automaton}

The previous sections introduced classical cellular automata, continuous extensions, and existing neural CA models. The following section focuses 
on the main theoretical components that form the basis of the three-dimensional neural cellular automaton used later in the thesis. The goal is to
clarify the general concepts without referring to specific implementation details.

\subsection{State Representation}

In neural cellular automata, each voxel holds a vector of real-valued channels. This concept originates from the 2D model of Mordvintsev 
et al.~\cite{Mordvintsev2020}, where multi-channel states support both visible pattern information and internal memory.

The channels typically fall into two categories:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Visible channels}, which represent quantities compared directly to the target pattern (for example colour, density, or occupancy),
    \item \textbf{Hidden channels}, which provide additional internal memory and facilitate communication across neighbours.
\end{itemize}

If each cell has $C$ channels, its state can be written as
\[
    \mathbf{s} \in \mathbb{R}^{C}.
\]

Many neural CA models also allocate a continuous \emph{alive} channel, which acts as a gating mechanism controlling whether updates are applied 
to the cell. This mechanism appears in earlier NCA work~\cite{Mordvintsev2020} and has conceptual links to continuous CA such as 
SmoothLife~\cite{Rafler2011} and Lenia~\cite{Chan2019}, where real-valued states enable smooth evolution and gradient-based optimisation.

\subsection{Perception Mechanism}

Despite using continuous state vectors, neural CA retain the defining CA property of locality: each cell updates based solely on information 
from its neighbourhood~\cite{IlachinskiCellular, Wolfram2002}. In neural CA, locality is achieved by applying a fixed set of convolutional filters
(e.g., 3$\times$3 in 2D or 3$\times$3$\times$3 in 3D) to extract local spatial features~\cite{Mordvintsev2020, Sudhakaran2021}.

Common filter types include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Identity filters}, capturing a cell's own state,
    \item \textbf{Neighbour aggregation filters}, such as sums or averages of face-adjacent neighbours,
    \item \textbf{Gradient filters}, including Sobel-type kernels extended to 3D,
    which approximate local directional differences and parallel biological gradient sensing~\cite{Mordvintsev2020, Sudhakaran2021}.
\end{itemize}

The outputs of all applied filters are concatenated into a single perception vector for each cell, which is passed to the update function.
Using fixed (non-learnable) filters helps stabilise training and preserves the conceptual continuity between neural and classical 
cellular automata~\cite{Mordvintsev2020, Chan2019}.

\subsection{Update Rule}

The update rule in neural cellular automata is a shared, parametric function that determines how each cell's state evolves over time. 
Following the formulation introduced by Mordvintsev et al.~\cite{Mordvintsev2020}, the rule operates on the perceived neighbourhood 
features and produces a state increment that is added to the current state:
\[
\mathbf{s}_{t+1} = \mathbf{s}_t + \Delta \mathbf{s}.
\]
This formulation preserves the parallel and local nature of classical cellular automata while allowing the transition function to be 
learned through gradient-based optimisation.

The update function is typically realized as a lightweight neural network applied independently to every cell, such as a small multilayer 
perceptron or a sequence of $1 \times 1$ (or $1 \times 1 \times 1$ in 3D) convolutions~\cite{Mordvintsev2020}. These architectures ensure 
that the update rule depends only on locally perceived information while keeping the parameter count low enough to maintain stable training.

Two mechanisms commonly appear across neural CA formulations. Alive gating uses a continuous ``alive'' signal 
to modulate whether a cell applies the update, providing a soft analogue of classical alive/dead states while 
improving long-term stability~\cite{Mordvintsev2020}. Stochastic update masking introduces a probabilistic update 
rate (or ``fire rate'') into the execution of the update rule, helping to prevent rigid synchronisation and 
encouraging robustness~\cite{Mordvintsev2020}.

These design elements support the emergence of behaviours such as growth, pattern persistence, and regeneration, phenomena also studied in 
continuous morphogenetic systems like Lenia~\cite{Chan2019}. Recent three-dimensional neural CA models~\cite{Rossi2021,Sudhakaran2021,Aadityaza2024} 
extend the same principles to volumetric grids, demonstrating that the underlying update-rule formulation generalises naturally beyond 2D.

\subsection{Grid Management}

The grid provides the spatial substrate on which the neural CA evolves.
It is typically a finite 3D lattice, although different boundary conditions may be used
depending on the experiment~\cite{Mordvintsev2020}.

Important considerations include lattice dimensions and boundary conditions, which are often fixed at sizes such as $32^3$ with 
periodic or zero-padding boundaries to avoid edge artefacts~\cite{Niklasson2021}. Initialization typically involves seeding the grid 
with one or a few active voxels, occasionally with added noise to improve robustness~\cite{Mordvintsev2020}. The simulation loop 
iterates the perception and update steps over multiple time steps, with batched or pooled simulations used to improve training efficiency~\cite{Chan2019}.

Three-dimensional grids are computationally demanding due to the cubic scaling of voxel-based environments. To address this, research on large-scale 
volumetric simulations frequently employs data structures designed to reduce memory usage and accelerate neighbourhood access. Common strategies include 
sparse voxel hierarchies, such as Sparse Voxel Octrees~\cite{Laine2010} and the OpenVDB framework~\cite{Museth2013}. These approaches demonstrate how 
locality-preserving representations can substantially improve scalability in volumetric systems and provide conceptual pathways for extending neural 
cellular automata to larger or more adaptive 3D environments.

\subsection{Training and Optimisation}

Because neural CA are differentiable, they can be optimised using gradient-based methods to minimise a loss between the generated pattern and a 
target configuration~\cite{Mordvintsev2020}. Training typically involves unrolling the CA dynamics over a number of steps and applying 
backpropagation through time.

Common loss functions include mean squared error (MSE) for continuous targets and intersection-over-union or other domain-specific metrics for 
structural tasks~\cite{Mordvintsev2020, Niklasson2021}. The choice of training regime-growth, persistence, or regeneration strongly influences the emergent 
behaviour and robustness of the model. Optimisation typically employs the Adam algorithm with learning rates around 0.001, often supplemented by early 
stopping, data pooling, or logging frameworks~\cite{Sudhakaran2021}.

These training strategies allow the discovery of update rules capable of producing stable and robust self-organising structures in both 2D 
and early 3D systems~\cite{Rossi2021,Sudhakaran2021,Aadityaza2024}.
